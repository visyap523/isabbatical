About Me

Tech Career

Since you wanted more, you get the long version.

I've worked in the tech industry since 2012.

My first corporate job was a product internship at the mobile gaming company GREE. I worked a game called Dino Life and learned gamification tactics (people will pay a lot for a rainbow stegosaurus).

Post-college I joined Guidebook (no-code mobile apps for events) as employee #26. I launched our higher-ed vertical, then our London-based EMEA operations, as a Customer Success Associate. Then I originated the Product Marketing function, working on sales enablement and strategy, before shifting into Product Management.

I was at Guidebook for 4.5+ years, and I consider that where I "grew up" in tech: a truly scrappy environment, in the rose-tinted 2010s sense of that word. Late nights with Thai food, flipcup at office parties, calling the CTO when our site crashed, soul-crushing product rollouts, heroic acts of service from beloved teammates, and learning every function from scratch.

I needed a change, so I applied to business school. Because I was in SF, a lot of people told me an MBA was useless. I was also sheepish because I knew it would make me Jared from Silicon Valley, but I did ultimately want it, for three reasons:

  • To get better at business strategy. Like, I legitimately wanted to learn more about the nuts and bolts of business and break down how companies work.
  • To effectively hold my own against executives and founders. Despite working closely with C-suite, I had impostor syndrome around them. I wanted to fix that.
  • To meet people outside of the Silicon Valley Bubble.

I got rejected. I tried again a year later, and was accepted into Harvard Business School for fall 2018. HBS was great. I had a better time than expected. If you want to know more about my experience there, here's an essay on why I chose an MBA over an MFA in Creative Writing.

In summer 2019 I interned at Duolingo in Pittsburgh, where I worked on Monetization, specifically the question: "Why do people upgrade to Plus?" One of my features (Hard Mode) drove +$2.5k in daily revenue during the experiment phase. I enjoyed Duo's design-forward, experiment-driven culture, and applying the knowledge from my mobile game days. Alas, I also really, really wanted to move to New York.

In 2020 I graduated, then joined the seed-stage start-up Thrilling (Etsy but for vintage clothing, which was crucial during covid). I was the third technical hire, after our CTO and one fullstack dev. I'd describe my role there as 50% Product, 50% Operations/whatever the CEO needed. This involved maturing our tech stack (implementing Zoom, Lever, Intercom, analytics, etc), and hiring. (A lot of hiring.) It was eye-opening to be part of the Series A fundraise, and to build a team from scratch. But part of me really wanted to join a later-stage org, and see how I'd fare in that environment.

In 2021 I joined Klaviyo's Data Infrastructure team, working on Profiles and Segmentation. My primary focus was on improving our segment builder. Over time, my team and I converted the UI to React/our design system; added in segment preview, cloning, additional operators, support for multiple where clauses; and fully upgraded the underlying infrastructure, drastically improving speed for our largest accounts, and decreasing overall costs.

I also worked on AI/ML features, launching our RFM segments and dashboard, before expanding my coverage in Data Infra. In the last 2 years I worked across the Data Platform (Data Warehouse/Data Lake/Online Analytics), supporting infra teams with a focus on enabling flexible queries for analytics features.

Klaviyo was the first place where I worked on teams that were entirely backend-focused. It was fun and fulfilling to drive technical decisions for foundational parts of our app, and collaborate closely with our (mostly internal) customers. There's more I want to learn in this area, so I'm spending part of my sabbatical studying how data-intensive applications are made.

Me as a Product Manager

I believe the core of the PM role boils down to the following:

  • Deeply understand your customers, their problems, and what makes them happy.
  • Unblock your teams, so they can solve customer problems as effectively as possible within your context (limited resources, company politics, etc).

A lot of the value I add as a PM focuses on those two areas. I'm happiest when I'm building actual solutions. There are periods when the job becomes mostly management/comms. I can do it, but I get kinda grumpy if it goes on too long.

Where I spike as a PM:

  • Customer research and interactions. I use my Customer Success/Product Marketing background heavily. I'm great at running my own interviews and drawing insights to inform things like ICP, product decisions, and roadmaps.
  • Communication. I'm comfortable presenting, and can talk across functions, roles, levels.
  • Documentation. I take a lot of pride in my note-taking (yes, even in our AI world), and writing stuff down so that work doesn't slip through the cracks, and decisions don't shift between meetings.
  • Collaborating with engineering. You will not need to argue with me on the importance of paying down technical debt or refactoring key components. I too want to build stable, functional products, with codebases you're happy to work in. I too want thoughtful rollouts and the ability to iterate. My favorite dev/PM working relationships are built on mutual trust where we seek the best solution, together.
  • Design. I care about the details. I care about how things look. I love the design and iteration process.
  • Figuring out whatever's needed to get things done.
